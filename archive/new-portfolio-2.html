Aviation SaaS · Shipped Product

FoxTrot: How design won a national enterprise client.

Legacy dispatch software was burying mission-critical aircraft data under generic SaaS UI. I rebuilt the entire information hierarchy around scannability and delivered a working frontend in 4 weeks.

My role
UI Designer + Frontend Dev
Timeline
4 weeks
Tools
Figma · Bootstrap · AngularJS
Outcome
Enterprise client won
↗ Executive summary

Problem: Off-the-shelf aviation management software displayed maintenance status and scheduling data with identical visual weight — dispatchers couldn't triage at a glance.


Outcome: Colour-coded status borders and a scannable data table architecture. The client won a major national flight school from an entrenched competitor. The design was cited in the sales pitch.

The insight: dispatchers don't browse, they scan

Competitive analysis revealed every incumbent platform treated maintenance and scheduling data as equivalent — long tables with uniform row heights and text-only status labels. User interviews showed dispatchers needed to assess fleet health while their eyes were focused elsewhere. The solution: thick colour-coded left borders readable at the edge of peripheral vision, with status badges that escalated in visual weight from "airworthy" to "grounded."

Process

01
Competitive teardown

Audited 4 competing platforms against real dispatcher tasks. Documented 23 specific friction points around status visibility and scheduling conflicts.

02
Information architecture redesign

Restructured from a "flat list of records" to a "triage hierarchy" — critical items surface first, not alphabetically.

03
High-fidelity + coded delivery

Built the frontend directly in Bootstrap + custom CSS. Collaborated daily with the AngularJS team for real-time data binding.

04
Client pitch integration

Demoed live in the enterprise sales pitch. The prospect cited the maintenance view scannability as the deciding factor.

Outcomes

4wk
Concept to shipped frontend

What I'd do differently

E-commerce · Shipped · A/B Tested

Checkout Funnel: From 2.1% to 3.4% conversion in 6 weeks.

A D2C supplement brand's checkout was losing £340K annually. Through friction auditing and a redesign validated with a 22,000-session A/B test, we recovered £218K in year one.

My role
Lead Product Designer
Timeline
6 weeks
Tools
Figma · Hotjar · Optimizely
Confidence
97.3% statistical
↗ Executive summary

Problem: Hotjar session recordings showed rage-clicks at address entry (forced account creation) and 31% abandonment at the card field.


Solution: Guest checkout as default, progressive address autocomplete, single-page layout with inline trust signals. Validated at 97.3% confidence over 22,847 sessions.

The 5 friction points we fixed

01
Forced account creation

47% of new users abandoned at the account gate. Replaced with guest checkout as primary CTA; account creation offered post-purchase with a discount incentive.

02
Manual address entry

Average completion: 68s, 4.2 errors/session. Added Google Places autocomplete — dropped to 11s, 0.3 errors.

03
3-page sprawl

Each page transition cost ~8% of users. Collapsed to a single-page accordion with persistent order summary.

04
Missing trust signals

Added security badges, return policy reminder, and social proof directly above the payment CTA.

05
Vague error messages

"Invalid card" → "Your card number should be 16 digits — you've entered 15." Field-specific, actionable.

Results

+62%
Relative conversion lift
£218K
Annual revenue recovered
97.3%
Statistical confidence

What I'd do differently

"I should have segmented by device from day one. Mobile and desktop converted at meaningfully different rates — a mobile-specific variant would likely have pushed the lift higher. Now queued as the next test."

— David Mercer, self-reflection

Meridian DS: A design system that cut dev time by 40%.

A 120-person fintech had 11 products with no shared visual language. I led the 0→1 design system from token architecture through 100% cross-product adoption in 8 months.

My role
Design Systems Lead
Timeline
8 months
Tools
Figma Variables · Storybook · React
Adoption
11/11 products
↗ Executive summary

Problem: 3 days per sprint lost to UI inconsistency. A single button had 23 different implementations. 11 separate Figma files with no shared components.


Outcome: 86 components, 340 design tokens, full Storybook documentation, 100% product adoption in 8 months. 40% reduction in UI implementation time per sprint.

Key architecture decisions

01
Token-first, not component-first

3-tier token system (primitive → semantic → component) using Figma Variables. Any brand update flows through all 86 components automatically.

02
Audit before building

3 weeks auditing all 11 products before writing a single component. Found 23 button variants, reduced to 4. Found 14 modal patterns, reduced to 2.

03
Dogfood on the hardest product first

Piloted with Payments — the most complex. Built credibility with engineering leads who were initially sceptical.

04
Office hours drove adoption

Weekly 30-min open sessions for any designer or engineer. Reduced Slack noise, built cross-squad relationships, surfaced real edge cases.

What I'd do differently

"I underestimated the politics of naming. I'd run a naming workshop in week one now, before touching Figma. Bad names embed themselves and create adoption resistance later."

— David Mercer, self-reflection
