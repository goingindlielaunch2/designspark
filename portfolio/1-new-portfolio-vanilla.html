Bonus: Aesthetics as a UX Tool

Like fashion, UI aesthetics run in cycles. Aspects of the 70s or 80s re-appear in our clothing, just as the early 2000s web is influencing modern interfaces. But bringing back these aesthetics isn't just about nostalgia—it drastically impacts user behavior. By stepping away from the hyper-flat designs of the 2010s and re-introducing tactile depth, we lower cognitive weight. Interactive elements actually look interactive, improving user flow and engagement.

The "Neo-Aqua" Look

Inspired by early Mac OS. By using deep inner-shadows and glossy gradients, buttons invite the user to click. The high visual affordance means users don't have to guess if a shape is a button or just a label.

Neo-Brutalism

A reaction to over-polished corporate flat design. Characterized by high-contrast, harsh borders, and raw colors. It dominates Gen-Z tools because it cuts through the noise and establishes an unmistakable visual hierarchy.

Soft Skeuomorphism

Elements appear extruded from the background itself. While it must be used carefully due to contrast limits, its extreme softness reduces eye strain in high-density dashboards where flat lines would cause visual clutter.

CogniPlan: Designing a planner that respects executive dysfunction.

Most productivity tools fail ADHD users by demanding too much working memory just to set up a task. I led the design of CogniPlan, trading deep customization for immediate, visual actionability.

Executive Summary

The Problem: Competitor apps relied on complex taxonomy (tags, folders, nested lists), causing "setup overwhelm" that prevented neurodivergent users from actually starting their work.

The Impact: By restricting users to a single "Today View" and utilizing visual progress rings, we reduced time-to-first-task by 2.5x and improved 7-day retention by 42%.

Role
Lead Product Designer
Timeline
12 Weeks (2025)
Team
1 PM, 2 iOS Eng
Platform
Native iOS

Defining "Setup Overwhelm"

Current productivity apps (like Notion or Todoist) are incredibly powerful, but they require users to build their own systems. For individuals with ADHD, this creates a barrier known as executive dysfunction. The mental energy required to plan the task exhausts the energy needed to do the task.

"I spend 45 minutes organizing my tasks into perfectly color-coded folders, and by the time I'm done, I'm too mentally exhausted to actually start working." — User Interview #4

Business Constraint

We needed an MVP shipped in 3 months to secure seed funding. We couldn't build complex two-way calendar syncs right away; we had to focus purely on core habit formation mechanics.

Decision: Trading Customization for Clarity

Early wireframes included a traditional 7-day calendar. However, usability testing revealed that seeing a full week of tasks triggered "time blindness" and anxiety in our neurodivergent testers.

The Strategic Pivot: I made the controversial decision to remove the weekly view entirely from the MVP. Instead, we forced users into a "Today Only" paradigm.

Original Approach (Rejected)

  • Complex infinite scroll calendar
  • High cognitive load (anxiety inducing)
  • Required 5+ taps to create a task

Final Approach (Shipped)

  • Visual "Now / Next / Later" spatial blocks
  • Future tasks strictly hidden for present-focus
  • 1 tap to initiate a preset "micro-task"

Navigating Engineering Constraints

To make time visually tangible, I initially designed a feature where active task cards slowly drained of color as time passed. However, during a technical spike, engineering flagged that constant re-rendering of custom UI gradients on iOS was causing severe battery drain.

Instead of fighting for the "dribbble-perfect" animation, I collaborated with the iOS lead to find a native compromise. We replaced the draining gradient with native iOS circular progress rings combined with distinct haptic feedback at 15-minute intervals. It preserved the psychological "tangible time" feeling while maintaining 60fps performance and saving battery.

Outcomes & Reflections

42%
Increase in 7-day habit streaks among beta users.
2.5x
Faster "Time-to-first-task" setup compared to control.
180+
Qualitative feedback tickets praising "reduced anxiety".

Hindsight & Learnings

If I had more time before the MVP freeze, I would have invested earlier in accessibility testing for screen readers. Because we relied heavily on visual color cues, we initially neglected how those states would be announced via VoiceOver. I had to retroactively define aria-labels for the engineering team, which was a great learning moment regarding "shift-left" accessibility.

Lumina: Curing "Remediation Paralysis" in enterprise accessibility.

Digital accessibility is a legal requirement, but automated scanners overwhelm product teams with thousands of raw code errors. I designed a standalone SaaS product that translates these chaotic scans into prioritized, human-centric workflows.

Executive Summary

The Strategy: Flipped the standard Information Architecture from a "Flat List of URLs" to a "Journey-Based Hierarchy," allowing teams to fix critical user flows (like Checkout) before worrying about obscure footer links.

The Impact: By blueprinting the offline triage-to-Jira handoff and designing an intuitive dashboard, this concept reduces the time required for a Product Manager to triage an accessibility audit by an estimated 70%.

Role
Lead Product Designer
Methods
Service Blueprinting, IA
Focus
Accessibility (WCAG)
Context
Standalone SaaS Concept

The Emotional Job: Remediation Paralysis

In B2B software, getting hit with an accessibility audit is a high-stress event. When a Product Manager runs an automated WCAG scanner on an enterprise site, it often returns 10,000+ individual violations (missing aria-labels, contrast issues, trap focuses).

The emotional job here isn't just "showing the data." It is entirely about reducing the crushing anxiety of legal risk and unmanageable workload. Teams experience "remediation paralysis"—the list is so big, they don't know where to start, so they do nothing.

"The scanner told us we had 4,000 contrast errors. My devs looked at the spreadsheet, panicked, and said it would take 3 sprints just to verify them. We just gave up." — Hypothetical Target Persona (Enterprise PM)

Flipping the Information Architecture

Legacy tools use a Page-Based Architecture (e.g., "Here are all the errors on /about-us"). This is functionally useless for a business, because a broken button on the checkout page is infinitely more critical than a broken image tag in a 2018 blog post.

I completely restructured the IA to be Journey-Based.

Lumina ingests the scan data and maps it to core user flows (Onboarding, Search, Checkout). By changing the taxonomy, we immediately solve the prioritization problem. A PM can now see: "The 'Add to Cart' flow has 3 critical blocking errors. Fix those today."

Old IA (Flat List)

  • 1. /home (241 errors)
  • 2. /checkout (89 errors)
  • 3. /blog/post-1 (412 errors)
  • 4. /contact (12 errors)

New IA (Journey-Based)

  • Critical: Checkout Flow
  • Step 1: Payment (3 blockers)
  • Warning: Lead Gen Form
  • Low: Blog Archive

Service Blueprinting the Offline Handoff

Detecting an error is only 10% of the job. The real friction happens in the offline service blueprint:

  1. PM reviews the dashboard.
  2. PM manually copies code snippets into Jira.
  3. Developer asks for visual context.
  4. QA analyst has to manually re-test with a screen reader.

I mapped this exact workflow and designed Lumina to bridge these gaps. I designed a "One-Click Jira Ticket" feature that automatically bundles the DOM element, the WCAG guideline, and a visual screenshot of the violation into a developer-ready format, drastically reducing the offline back-and-forth.

100%
Alignment with WCAG 2.2 AA standards via actionable tickets.
Zero
Need for developers to learn complex accessibility auditing software.

Shift: Turning post-purchase regret into retained revenue.

Returns are the most frustrating part of online shopping and a multi-billion dollar leak for D2C brands. I designed a white-label returns portal that uses behavioral nudges to effortlessly convert refunds into instant exchanges.

Executive Summary

The Problem: The standard return flow forces users to find a printer, buy tape, and wait weeks for a refund. Because of this friction, users almost never exchange items; they just refund and churn.

The Impact: By introducing QR-code drop-offs and a "Bonus Credit" UI nudge right at the moment of decision, this product concept theoretically converts 38% of traditional refunds into retained revenue.

Role
Lead Product Designer
Core Metric
Revenue Retention %
Focus
Behavioral Psych
Context
SaaS Product Concept

The "Printer Friction" Trap

When a customer buys a pair of shoes that don't fit, they actually want the shoes—they just need a different size. But the traditional e-commerce return UX actively punishes them for trying to exchange.

I conducted a friction audit on top e-commerce brands and found a massive disconnect between the digital and physical experience. A user can buy an item with one tap (Apple Pay), but returning it requires finding a PDF, locating a physical printer, buying packing tape, and waiting in line at a post office.

"I wanted the medium sweater instead of the large, but printing the label was such a hassle I just returned it for cash. I ended up buying a different sweater from Amazon instead." — User Discovery Interview

Designing the Behavioral Nudge

The critical moment in a return flow is the fork in the road: Refund to Original Payment vs Exchange/Store Credit.

Most brands treat these equally in the UI as two plain radio buttons. I completely redesigned this decision matrix using behavioral economics (Loss Aversion and Instant Gratification).

The Standard UI
Refund to Visa ending in 1234
Get Store Credit

Result: 85% of users choose Refund.

The "Shift" UI
Exchange instantly +$10 Bonus
Ships today. No waiting.
Refund to Original Payment
Takes 7-10 business days.

Result: Users incentivized by immediate shipping.

Removing Physical Friction

Solving the UI wasn't enough; the physical drop-off was still a barrier. I designed the Printer-less Drop-off Flow.

Once a user selects their return, Shift generates a high-contrast QR code. The user simply brings the unboxed item to a partner location (like a pharmacy or post office). The clerk scans the QR code from the user's phone, takes the raw item, and handles the packing.

The Magic Moment: Because the system integrates with logistics APIs, the exact second the clerk scans the QR code, a webhook triggers the user's exchange order to ship, or releases their store credit instantly to their wallet. We removed days of anxiety and waiting from the feedback loop.

Aura: Curing invoice anxiety through personality and motion.

Traditional finance tools look like spreadsheets, making the already-awkward task of asking clients for money feel cold and corporate. I designed Aura, a freelance invoicing tool that uses vibrant branding and delightful micro-animations to give creators confidence.

Executive Summary

The Strategy: Treat brand personality and motion design not as "polish," but as core UX tools to solve the emotional friction of imposter syndrome and financial anxiety in independent workers.

The Impact: By implementing conversational UX, the "Gentle Nudge" feature, and dopamine-driven completion animations, the platform saw a 22% increase in on-time invoice payments compared to traditional PDFs.

Product & Motion Designer

The Emotional Toll of Asking for Money

When investigating the creator economy, I found that sending an invoice isn't a data-entry problem; it's a deeply emotional one. Freelancers, especially those early in their careers, battle intense imposter syndrome.

Traditional accounting tools (like QuickBooks or Xero) are designed for CFOs. They use sterile "corporate blue" interfaces, heavy data tables, and harsh terminology. This clinical environment exacerbates the user's anxiety, making them feel like they are initiating a legal confrontation rather than simply getting paid for their art.

"I will literally put off sending an invoice for three weeks because hitting 'Send' feels so aggressive. I don't want to ruin the relationship with my client." — Freelance Illustrator, User Interview

Brand Voice as a Functional UX Tool

To solve this, I deliberately stripped away the "fintech" aesthetic. I developed a design system for Aura that utilized a warm, optimistic color palette (Sunset Rose, Amber, and Soft Lavender) and soft, rounded UI geometries.

More importantly, I leveraged conversational UX. Instead of a form labeled Input Client Data, the interface asks, "Who are we celebrating today?" Instead of Net-30 Terms, the default copy reads, "When should they pop this in your account?"

By changing the structural framing from a "corporate demand" to a "collaborative milestone," the cognitive friction of filling out the invoice plummeted.

Motion that Informs and Delights

In Aura, micro-animations aren't just decorative confetti—they fulfill a core usability heuristic: Visibility of System Status. I used physics-based motion (via Lottie) to create moments of intense dopamine that reward the user for completing stressful tasks.

The "Send" Interaction

Instead of a static loading spinner, pressing "Send Invoice" triggers a micro-animation of a paper plane looping and soaring off-screen. It provides immediate, undeniable system feedback.

The "Paid" Celebration

When an invoice is paid, the status badge physically "pops" open, dropping three animated gold coins into a digital piggy bank. This visceral feedback turns a dry transaction into a victory.

Navigator: Designing trust into enterprise AI search.

Information silos cost enterprise companies millions in lost productivity. I designed Navigator—an OS-level, AI-powered command center that replaces frustrating keyword hunts with conversational, context-aware discovery.

Executive Summary

The Problem: Employees were experiencing high cognitive load context-switching between Confluence, Google Drive, and Slack just to find basic company policies, leading to repeated questions and bottlenecked workflows.

The Impact: By utilizing a globally accessible `Cmd+K` interface and designing an AI response system anchored heavily in verifiable citations, we reduced time-to-document by 3x and deflected 40% of repetitive IT/HR support tickets.

Role
Lead UX Designer
Core Metric
Time-to-Discovery
Architecture
RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Gen)
Platform
Desktop Overlay

The Emotional Toll of Information Silos

In modern enterprises, knowledge is fragmented. The onboarding manual is in Notion, the Q3 financials are in Google Drive, and the engineering architecture is buried in a Slack thread from eight months ago.

Through user interviews, I discovered that the frustration wasn't just about wasted time—it was about imposter syndrome. Employees felt incompetent when they had to repeatedly ask their managers for links they "should" already know how to find.

"I spend 20 minutes looking for a specific HR policy, give up, and then ping someone on Slack. I hate doing it because it interrupts their day and makes me look disorganized." — Mid-level Software Engineer, User Interview

Designing the "Command Center"

Traditional enterprise search is an independent web portal (e.g., SharePoint). This is a flawed UX paradigm because it requires a massive context switch. To find an answer, the user must leave their current flow, open a new tab, navigate to a portal, and formulate a boolean query.

I advocated for a globally accessible overlay approach, heavily inspired by Apple's Spotlight and Raycast.

By defining a global shortcut (`Cmd+K` / `Ctrl+K`), Navigator instantly summons a floating search interface over whatever application the user is currently in. This entirely eliminated the friction of context-switching, allowing users to query company data without leaving their IDE or email client.

Designing for AI Trust (Solving Hallucinations)

The core engine of Navigator is an LLM querying a vector database (RAG). However, users are acutely aware that AI hallucinates. If an AI gives an employee the wrong parental leave policy, it creates severe liability.

To solve the "Trust Barrier," I designed the UX strictly around Transparency and Verifiable Citations. The AI's generated summary is treated as secondary; the primary artifacts are the underlying source documents.

The "ChatGPT" Anti-Pattern

Just spitting out conversational text forces the user to blindly trust the machine. In an enterprise environment, un-cited AI output is viewed as dangerous and unreliable by power users.

The "Citation First" Pattern

Every sentence generated by Navigator features a highly visible, clickable pill [1] that anchors directly to the exact paragraph in Google Drive or Confluence where the data was sourced.

Optima: Democratizing UX Audits with Vision AI.

High-quality UX audits are a luxury usually reserved for enterprise budgets. I designed a SaaS platform that uses multi-modal AI (vision + text) to instantly scan a website, map heuristic violations, and prescribe exact UI fixes.

Executive Summary

The Problem: Small teams and startups cannot afford the $5k-$20k price tag of an agency UX audit. As a result, basic usability flaws (contrast, confusing hierarchy, dead clicks) directly drain their conversion rates.

The Impact: By translating Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics into an AI prompt structure, and designing a "Spatial Diagnostic" UI, Optima allows non-designers to uncover and action expert-level UX improvements in under 3 minutes.

Role
Principal Product Designer
Target Persona
Founders & Solo-Devs
Technology
Multi-Modal LLMs
Context
SaaS Proof of Concept

The "UX Maturity" Gap

Good design is systemic, but it's often treated as subjective. When non-designers build software, they frequently violate basic laws of human-computer interaction (HCI) without realizing it—burying critical actions, breaking visual hierarchies, or providing zero system feedback.

I conceptualized Optima to bridge this gap. The goal was not to replace senior designers, but to automate the baseline. By grounding a Vision LLM in Jakob Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics and the WCAG accessibility guidelines, we could automate the discovery of foundational flaws.

Designing Spatial Diagnostics

The hardest challenge in designing AI tools is the presentation of data. If the AI simply generates a text document saying, "Your primary button is hard to read," the user has to guess which button it means.

I designed a Spatial Diagnostic Interface. When a user pastes a URL, Optima renders a high-fidelity image of the site. The UI uses coordinate-mapping from the LLM to draw glowing, interactive bounding boxes directly over the offending UI elements.

The Text Output UX (Flawed)

Reading a long bulleted list of UX issues lacks context. The cognitive load required to map a text description back to a visual interface creates massive friction for the user.

The Spatial Output UX (Solved)

Hovering over "Issue #3" in the sidebar physically highlights the corresponding "Add to Cart" button on the preview canvas. Context is immediate and undeniable.

FoxTrot: Task-optimized design for flight school management.

Aviation management involves time-critical data, high-density schedules, and rigorous safety maintenance tracking. I designed and developed a bespoke UI that cuts through the noise and provides absolute operational clarity.

Executive Summary

The Problem: Off-the-shelf management software lacked the specialized workflows required for aviation. Data density was too high, making it difficult for dispatchers to assess aircraft maintenance statuses and student schedules at a glance.

The Impact: By utilizing user-focused visual cues and a highly customized Bootstrap/AngularJS frontend, I delivered an application that prioritized scannability and action—directly resulting in the acquisition of a large national client from competitors.

Role
UI Designer & Frontend Dev
Timeline

Tools

Final High-Fidelity Dashboard: Maintenance & Schedule Views

The Approach: Designing for Scannability

In an aviation dispatch environment, users don't "browse" software; they extract mission-critical data in seconds. The strategy centered heavily on competitive analysis, determining where legacy systems failed to provide immediate visual clarity.

I designed the UI with an extreme emphasis on scannability. Instead of relying solely on text to convey aircraft status, I implemented strong, user-focused visual cues. For example, the maintenance table utilizes thick, color-coded left borders (deep red for critical, amber for warnings) ensuring that dispatchers can assess fleet health peripherally while focusing on scheduling.

Technical Implementation & Architecture

Because the data (aircraft status, instructor certifications, and real-time flight schedules) required constant, dynamic updates without page refreshes, the engineering team utilized AngularJS.

My role was to bridge the gap between static design (Photoshop) and functional front-end code. I built the responsive foundation using the Bootstrap grid system to ensure robust layout consistency across devices. However, I layered in extensive custom HTML/CSS to completely override Bootstrap's default "off-the-shelf" aesthetic.

The Grid as a Tool, Not a Constraint

By utilizing custom CSS on top of Bootstrap, we achieved a highly bespoke interface tailored explicitly for high-density data. We tightened padding, implemented bespoke typography scales, and created custom data-table components that felt distinctly premium and "aviation-grade," moving far beyond standard generic SaaS templates.
